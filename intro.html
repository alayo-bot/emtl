Understanding Emergency Management
& the Power of Maps

How societies prepare for catastrophe — and why knowing where is just as critical as knowing what.

Section 01

What Is Emergency Management?

Emergency management is the organized effort to reduce the vulnerability of communities to hazards and to cope with disasters when they occur. It is a continuous cycle — not a single moment of crisis response — built on four interdependent phases that governments, agencies, and communities work through before, during, and after any emergency.

Mitigation

Reducing long-term risk before disaster strikes through land-use planning, building codes, and public education.

Preparedness

Planning, training, and equipping responders and communities to act effectively when an incident occurs.

Response

Immediate actions taken during an incident to save lives, protect property, and meet basic human needs.

Recovery

Restoring affected communities to normal conditions, addressing physical, economic, and social impacts.

Section 02

Why Maps Matter in Every Phase

Disasters are fundamentally spatial events. A hurricane makes landfall at a location. A wildfire spreads across terrain. A toxic spill follows a watershed. Without maps, decision-makers are navigating a crisis blind — unable to visualize where the threat is, who is at risk, or where resources should go.

01

Situational Awareness — Seeing the Whole Picture

Maps integrate disparate data streams — weather, road closures, shelter locations, hospital capacity — into a single shared operating picture that allows commanders to make rapid, informed decisions during the chaos of an active incident.

02

Hazard Identification — Knowing What's at Risk

Flood inundation maps, wildfire risk overlays, and earthquake shake maps allow planners to identify vulnerable populations and critical infrastructure before a disaster, enabling targeted mitigation investments where they matter most.

03

Resource Deployment — Getting Help Where It's Needed

Spatial analysis calculates optimal routes for emergency vehicles, proximity of fire stations to incident zones, and locations for staging areas — turning logistics from guesswork into geographically optimized precision.

04

Evacuation Planning — Moving People to Safety

Maps model traffic flow, identify contraflow corridors, pinpoint shelter locations relative to population density, and highlight communities with limited mobility — making the difference between orderly evacuation and gridlocked chaos.

05

Damage Assessment — Measuring the Impact

Post-disaster aerial and satellite imagery, combined with parcel data and building footprints, lets recovery teams rapidly survey damage extents across large areas, prioritizing communities with the greatest need for federal assistance.

All disasters are local — and all local disasters have a location. Maps are the language through which emergency managers communicate urgency, allocate resources, and ultimately save lives. — Core Principle of Geographic Emergency Management
